High School Intellectual Property Alliance

Teaching students how ideas become impact.

The High School Intellectual Property Alliance helps students build IP clubs that make patents, trademarks, copyright, trade secrets, licensing, and innovation policy understandable before college. We started at Stevenson. Now we are building a national network of chapters for students who create, research, design, code, compete, publish, and lead.

Founding chapter Stevenson IP Club is the proof of concept: a student-led club that treats intellectual property as a practical skill for young inventors, researchers, artists, entrepreneurs, and advocates.

Why this exists

Students are already creating. They should understand what creation means.

  • 1 A student can build an app, publish research, design a brand, or invent a device without ever learning who owns the work.
  • 2 IP decisions affect public health, artificial intelligence, music, startups, engineering, fashion, sports, and science policy.
  • 3 High schools need a clear, student-run way to teach the rules and responsibilities behind innovation.

A national alliance with a specific mission.

HSIPA is not a general business club or a one-time speaker series. It is a chapter system for teaching intellectual property as a civic, creative, and technical subject that high school students can use.

01

Make IP accessible before college

We translate core IP concepts into high school programming without watering them down. Students learn vocabulary, examples, and decision-making frameworks they can apply to real projects.

02

Give student leaders a real structure

Each chapter can run meetings, workshops, case discussions, guest speaker events, and school outreach using a shared model that still leaves room for local leadership.

03

Connect schools to a broader movement

A national alliance helps chapters share materials, invite mentors, compare projects, and make intellectual property education visible beyond a single school.

Four tracks that make IP feel real.

Chapters can mix these tracks across meetings, workshops, competitions, and guest events. The point is not memorization. The point is judgment.

Patents

Invention and engineering

Prior art, invention disclosure, patent claims, lab notebooks, prototypes, and how technical ideas become protected assets.

Brands

Trademarks and identity

Naming, logos, consumer confusion, school ventures, creator brands, and what makes a mark distinctive.

Creative work

Copyright and authorship

Music, writing, art, photography, software, fair use, attribution, and the rights students already interact with online.

Strategy

Licensing and public impact

Ownership, collaboration, startups, research transfer, open source, access, and how IP choices shape the world people live in.
